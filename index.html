🧬 Peptide Deep Analysis

This peptide follows a classic antimicrobial design pattern using positively charged and hydrophobic amino acids.

Charge: Positively charged residues like lysine (K) allow the peptide to bind to negatively charged bacterial membranes. This is critical because bacteria have more negatively charged surfaces than human cells.

Hydrophobicity: Hydrophobic amino acids like leucine (L) allow the peptide to insert into the lipid membrane, destabilizing it.

Amphipathic structure: The alternating K and L pattern creates a structure where one side interacts with water and the other with the membrane.

Why this matters: Effective antimicrobial peptides must both bind and disrupt membranes. This peptide achieves both.
