🧬 Peptide Deep Analysis

This peptide follows a classic antimicrobial design pattern using positively charged and hydrophobic residues.

Charge: Lysine (K) provides strong positive charge, allowing the peptide to bind to negatively charged bacterial membranes. This selectivity is important because human cells are less negatively charged.

Hydrophobicity: Leucine (L) allows the peptide to insert into the lipid bilayer of the membrane. This is essential for disrupting membrane integrity.

Amphipathic structure: The alternating K and L pattern allows the peptide to form a structure where one side is hydrophilic (interacts with water) and the other is hydrophobic (interacts with membranes).

Why this matters: Without both charge and hydrophobicity, the peptide would either fail to bind or fail to disrupt the membrane.
